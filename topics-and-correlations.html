Different chats, different jobs. When a customer starts a chat it is mostly address & directions (32.6%) plus contact and delivery preferences. When a rider starts a chat it is overwhelmingly rider arrival (60.7%). Only rider arrival and other appear high in both lists; every other reason is essentially customer-driven.

Real customer messages — "Address & directions" instructions

Actual unaltered customer messages (original text), top 20 by frequency over 12 months, for the #1 customer-started reason. The count after each is how many times that exact message was sent.

Customers are filling the gaps the stored address leaves: floor / level, a bell instruction (ring or don't ring), and a unit identifier (villa / gate / building). This is exactly the granularity missing from the app address — and why "unable to find" dominates the cancellations on these chats.

Read the columns: Rider chats are almost all arrival announcements; Customer chats spread across address, contact, payment and preferences. Cancelled orders light up on cancellation (16× vs average) and go dark on address & directions and leave at door. Night over-indexes on don't ring and cancellation; morning on leave at door. Weekday/weekend columns are almost all flat — the visual proof that day of week doesn't matter.

4. Who starts the chat decides the topic

Share of chats within each initiator group. The strongest pattern in the study (V=0.61).

5. Which factors correlate with the topic — strength

Cramér's V (0–1): how strongly each factor moves topic mix, all on one identical base of 2,672,704 chats. ~0.1 small · ~0.3 medium · ~0.5+ large.

Who starts the chat dominates everything (V=0.61). Whether the order was cancelled is the next strongest (V=0.24). Time of day and order type are small; weekday vs weekend barely matters (V=0.02).
