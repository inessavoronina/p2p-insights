Topic share within each outcome group

How the topic mix differs between the two groups (each group sums to 100%). Association V=0.237.

Of all chats, only ~3.4% sit on cancelled orders — but that rate swings sharply by reason. cancellation chats are 52.9% on cancelled orders, WhatsApp / alt contact 7.1%, item / order problem 5.4%, while leave at door (0.9%) and don't ring (1.1%) almost never appear on cancelled orders — those happen only once the rider has reached the door.

Customer-started chats only: topic mix, cancelled vs delivered

Difference in topic share (cancelled % − delivered %), customer-initiated chats only. Right / red = more common on cancelled orders; left / green = more common on delivered. Numbers on the right show the gap and (cancelled% / delivered%). Association V=0.334 — stronger than the all-chats 0.237.

Two mirror-image signals dominate customer chats: cancelled-order chats are 21% explicit cancellation language (vs 0.5% on delivered), while delivered-order chats are 33% address & directions (vs 11%). The split is clean — distress topics (cancellation, ETA/urgency, item problems, WhatsApp) skew to cancelled orders; successful last-mile coordination (address, leave-at-door, don't-ring) skews to delivered, because those only happen once the rider has reached the door.

Rider-started chats only: topic mix, cancelled vs delivered

Same view for rider-initiated chats, on the same scale as above. The bars are far smaller — association V=0.113, only about a third of the customer effect (0.334).

For rider-started chats, topic barely predicts outcome. Riders announce arrival (~57–61%) either way; the only small nudges are slightly less "rider arrival" and slightly more "cancellation" / "other" on cancelled orders. Unlike customers, riders don't produce a distinct "distress" topic mix — so the customer's chat is the far better early signal of a cancellation.

Different chats, different jobs. When a customer starts a chat it is mostly address & directions (32.6%) plus contact and delivery preferences. When a rider starts a chat it is overwhelmingly rider arrival (60.7%). Only rider arrival and other appear high in both lists; every other reason is essentially customer-driven.

Real customer messages — "Address & directions" instructions

Actual unaltered customer messages (original text), top 20 by frequency over 12 months, for the #1 customer-started reason. The count after each is how many times that exact message was sent.

Customers are filling the gaps the stored address leaves: floor / level, a bell instruction (ring or don't ring), and a unit identifier (villa / gate / building). This is exactly the granularity missing from the app address — and why "unable to find" dominates the cancellations on these chats.

Inside "Other / short text" — real customer messages

Actual unaltered customer messages that fell into the catch-all "other" bucket, top 22 by frequency. The count after each is how many times that exact message was sent.

"Other" is not a hidden topic — it's mostly social glue: greetings ("Hi", "Hello", "السلام عليكم"), acknowledgements ("Ok", "Yes") and thanks make up the bulk. But two real signals hide here: bare "?" / "??" / "???" (~28k) are customers poking an unresponsive rider, and short coordination words ("Coming", "Where are u", "Come inside", ~44k) actually belong in rider arrival / ETA & urgency — so those two reasons are slightly undercounted and "other" is slightly inflated.

WhatsApp is a location workaround, not a call channel. Riders ask for it to get a live location pin (54.5%); customers use it to send their exact location (and sometimes a delivery photo) because the app pin is too rough — e.g. "so I can send you the most accurate location of my house, as many drivers get lost". Voice calls are a minor intent (≤10%). It resolves the same "unable to find" problem as address chats — but moves the interaction, location and photos off-platform.

Why "Leave at door" cancellations are tagged "unprofessional behaviour"

For customer-started leave-at-door chats on cancelled orders, the top cancel reason is "unprofessional behaviour" (25%). Reading those chats explains why — and it isn't the chat itself.

The chats are polite — the customer just sets an explicit do/don't list:
It isn't the chat that's unprofessional — it's the broken expectation. Leave-at-door is the only topic where the customer hands the rider a contactless contract (don't ring, don't call, leave it, send a photo, sometimes "put it inside"). The violation happens in the physical world, not the chat: when the rider rings the bell on a sleeping baby, phones after being told not to, demands the customer come down, or doesn't leave it properly, the customer logs an "unprofessional behaviour" complaint after delivery (94% post-delivery). Other topics don't set such expectations, so they rarely trigger this reason — and because the rider reached the door, "unable to find" is absent, leaving post-delivery behaviour/quality as the main thing that can go wrong.

The cancel message is a notification, not a justification. 9 in 10 customers just say "cancel" — which fits the fact that the rider can't actually cancel the order (it goes through the app / support), so the customer is only informing the person they're chatting with. When a reason is given, it's led by impatience ("taking too long", 32%) and second thoughts ("changed mind", 29%) — not order or quality problems. This silence is also why so many cancellation chats land in ambiguous failure buckets: there's no customer-stated reason to record.

The headline: rider communication style barely separates the groups — and what differs points to reverse causality. High-cancel riders actually read more customer messages (53% vs 43%) and miss fewer chats (44% vs 55%). That's because hard deliveries generate more back-and-forth — the chatter is a symptom of trouble, not the cause. The cancel-rate gap (7.4×) is far larger than any comms-metric gap, so most of it is driven by delivery context (location difficulty), not by how the rider chats.

The one stylistic tell: "I can't find your address" ranks #4 for high-cancel riders but #7 for low-cancel — high-cancel riders get stuck on location more. Low-cancel riders' mix tilts toward completion and closure: "I will leave the order at the doorstep" ranks much higher, plus proactive arrival in the customer's language ("لقد وصلت" / "I have arrived") and "Thank you" — they announce, drop, confirm, close. Low-cancel riders also reply slightly faster on first contact (199s vs 216s).
Caveats. Cancel rate is measured only over orders that had a P2P chat (not the rider's full order book), so it over-weights problem deliveries. Rider identity comes from the chat sender_id. This is correlation, not causation — area/location difficulty is an unmeasured confounder, and the data can't say a rider would cancel less if they only changed how they chat.
